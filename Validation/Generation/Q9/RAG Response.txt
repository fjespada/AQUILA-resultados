**Service Decision: Service Decision pertains to the orchestration of quantum computational tasks within software frameworks.**

To decouple quantum functionalities and expose them as scalable, I recommend applying the following design patterns:

1. **Quantum Head Pattern**
	* Problem: Scalability and performance issues when integrating classical and quantum components.
	* Solution: Delegating inference tasks to a classical head, reducing data dimensionality, and using quantum components for specific computations.
	* Benefits: Improved scalability, maintainability, and performance.
	* Drawbacks: Increased complexity in integrating classical and quantum components.
2. **Quantum Intermediate Layer Pattern**
	* Problem: Handling large volumes of information within NISQ-era hardware constraints.
	* Solution: Delegating part of the inference task to a classical intermediate layer, reducing data dimensionality, and using quantum components for specific computations.
	* Benefits: Improved scalability, maintainability, and performance.
	* Drawbacks: Increased complexity in integrating classical and quantum components.

To concretely apply these patterns, refactor your code by:

1. Creating a separate service for orchestrating quantum computational tasks (Quantum Head Pattern).
2. Implementing a hybrid execution model that integrates classical and quantum components (Quantum Intermediate Layer Pattern).

By applying these design patterns, you can ensure that your quantum software system is scalable, maintainable, and efficient, while also addressing the unique constraints of NISQ-era hardware.

**Justification:**

* Scalability: By delegating tasks to a classical head or intermediate layer, you can improve scalability by reducing the load on quantum components.
* Maintainability: Separating concerns between classical and quantum components makes it easier to maintain and update individual components independently.
* Performance: Hybrid execution models can optimize performance by leveraging the strengths of both classical and quantum computing.
