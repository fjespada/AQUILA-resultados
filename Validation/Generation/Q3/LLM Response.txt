Traditional modular design can be challenging to apply to quantum software for several reasons:

Non-determinism: Quantum systems are inherently non-deterministic, meaning that small changes in initial conditions or parameters can lead to drastically different outcomes. Traditional modular design relies on determinism, making it difficult to scale to complex quantum algorithms.

Interconnectedness: Quantum systems often involve complex interactions between multiple qubits, which makes it challenging to decompose the system into separate modules. This interconnectedness requires a more holistic approach to design.

Sensitivity to noise and error correction: Quantum systems are extremely sensitive to noise and errors, which can be catastrophic for quantum computations. Traditional modular design often overlooks the importance of robustness against noise and errors.

Excessive abstraction in quantum software design can lead to several problems:

Loss of physical meaning: Over-abstraction can strip away the physical meaning behind the algorithms, making it difficult to interpret and understand the results.

Inability to optimize: Abstraction can make it challenging to optimize the algorithm for specific hardware or use cases, as the optimized solution may not be directly applicable to the physical system.

Difficulty in error correction: Excessive abstraction can obscure the underlying physics of the system, making it harder to implement effective error correction mechanisms.
