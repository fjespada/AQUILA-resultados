Meta-Solving is highly effective for integrating complex quantum features into classical architectures. It acts as an architectural routing pattern that systematically decomposes a large algorithmic problem into discrete subproblems, dynamically or statically assigning them to classical or quantum solvers based on their computational profiles.

Architecturally, this requires a strict Quantum-Classical Split. The classical infrastructure (CPU/Cloud) serves as the orchestrator: it encapsulates the Meta-Solving logic, manages control flow, and executes the solver selection mechanism. The quantum processor (QPU) functions strictly as a delegated coprocessor for specific subroutines where quantum advantage is anticipated.

To implement this without tightly coupling the classical application to quantum mechanics, the logic should be packaged within a Hybrid Module. This module exposes a unified Classical-Quantum Interface that accepts standard classical data and behavior parameters, abstracting the underlying complexity.

Crucially, for this architecture to be NISQ-viable, the Meta-Solving orchestration must account for severe physical constraints. The decomposition strategy must minimize cross-domain data transfer, network latency, and QPU queueing times, ensuring that the communication overhead between classical and quantum solvers does not negate the computational benefits
